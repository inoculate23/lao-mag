Personal Correspondence · April 2026
Dear Kao,

You were one of the first Lao people I ever spoke with. I don't know if you know what that means — probably you don't, because you were just being yourself, which is to say warm and generous and full of stories about a place I hadn't been yet. You gave me northern Laos before I arrived there. The way light moves on the hills in the early morning. The food. The particular quality of life in a small village where everyone knows what the river is doing. You handed me those things the way you'd hand someone a cup of something good, without ceremony, just because it was there and it was yours to give.

We didn't speak of the war. I want you to know I noticed that, and I understood it, and I wasn't going to be the one to bring it there. Some things don't need to be handed around over dinner. But I carried it with me when I traveled — especially to the Plain of Jars, especially to Xieng Khouang — and I felt the full weight of it there in a way I hadn't expected.

I visited the MAG office. The people who work to clear the unexploded bombs from the fields, one field at a time, fifty years on. I stood in their museum for a long time. I am not going to describe what I felt there, because you already know what is in that museum better than I do, even if you've never been inside it. You know it from the ground up. I know it from the outside in. Those are different kinds of knowing and I don't want to confuse them.

What I want to tell you is what I did afterward. I came home and I made something. Working with an AI collaborator — a tool and something more than a tool, it's hard to explain — I wrote and recorded a song about the bombs. About the fact that most people where I live don't know this happened. About the eighty million that are still in the soil. About the people who kneel in the same dirt and listen for what's sleeping, one village at a time. I called it Still in the Ground.

There are two versions. The first is the song. The second weaves in the actual words of survivors — a farmer, a student, a former monk, a fourteen-year-old boy — each spoken, from a book called Voices from the Plain of Jars. I want you to hear both. Not because I need you to approve them or tell me they are right — you don't owe me that — but because you were the door I walked through. Your stories were part of what made me go. And you should have the songs.

All proceeds go to MAG. That much I can do.
